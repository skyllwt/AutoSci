## Problem

Ubiquitylation is the second most abundant post-translational modification after phosphorylation, and ubiquitin ligases (E3s) provide its substrate specificity. E3s control the stability, localization and complex assembly of nearly every cellular protein, including tumour suppressors and oncoproteins. When E3 expression or activity is deregulated by genetic, epigenetic, or post-translational mechanisms in cancer, downstream substrate pools shift in ways that can either drive or restrain tumorigenesis depending on cellular context. Despite an obvious therapeutic opportunity, the field lacks a coherent mechanistic framework that connects E3 deregulation to specific malignant phenotypes (genomic instability, dysregulated signalling, metabolic rewiring) and to actionable targets, especially given that the same E3 (for example, [[ubiquitin-ligase-e3]] complexes such as SCF–FBXW7 or SPOP) can be tumour-suppressive or oncogenic in different lineages.

## Key idea

The review organizes the cancer-relevant E3 landscape around a single argument: an E3's role in cancer is determined not by the ligase itself but by the substrate set it engages in a given cellular context. Genetic, epigenetic and post-translational alterations rewire which substrates an E3 ubiquitylates, which chain topology it builds (K48 vs K63 vs mono-/di-ubiquitylation), and where in the cell the reaction occurs. Three consequences follow: (1) the same E3 can switch between oncogenic and tumour-suppressor roles in different lineages or signalling states; (2) the most tractable therapeutic angles are not "inhibit the E3" but "restore or perturb a specific E3–substrate axis" (for example, MDM2–p53, SCF–FBXW7–cyclin E, or VHL–HIF); (3) the next generation of cancer drugs should exploit ubiquitylation directly — as small-molecule disruptors of E3–substrate interactions, as proteasome modulators, or as PROTAC-style targeted protein degraders that hijack a healthy E3 to remove an otherwise undruggable oncoprotein. See [[ubiquitylation]] for the underlying enzymatic cascade.

## Method

This is a narrative review synthesizing roughly two decades of biochemistry, mouse genetics, structural biology, and clinical pharmacology around E3-substrate axes implicated in cancer. The authors:

- enumerate cancer-relevant E3 families (HECT, RING, RBR; cullin–RING ligases including SCF–FBXW7, SCF–SKP2, SCF–β-TRCP, CRL3–SPOP, CRL2–VHL; APC/C–CDC20 and APC/C–CDH1; the p53 ligase MDM2; the BRCA1–BARD1 heterodimer; parkin/PARK2; NEDD4; RNF8/RNF138; and others) and tabulate their substrates and cancer associations in supplementary tables.
- compile mouse models in which the E3 has been knocked out, knocked in as a missense allele, or conditionally deleted (TABLE 1), and read off what each genetic perturbation does to tumour incidence and spectrum.
- cluster the E3-substrate axes by the malignant phenotype they affect: cell cycle and proliferation (for example, FBXW7–cyclin E, APC/C–CDC20, SCF–SKP2–p27); DNA damage repair (MDM2–p53, BRCA1–BARD1, RNF8/RNF138 chromatin signalling); signal transduction (NEDD4–RAS/PTEN, BRAF ubiquitylation by RNF149 and CDH1, SCF–SKP2-mediated AKT activation, FBXW7–mTOR).
- map onto each axis the candidate or approved drug modality (Nutlin/RG7112 for MDM2–p53; SCF–SKP2 inhibitors; PARP inhibitors that exploit BRCA1/HR deficiency; PROTACs as a general E3-recruiting strategy).

No new experiments are reported; the review's contribution is the framework and the consolidated mapping.

## Results

Key results synthesized across the literature:

- E3 deregulation is pervasive in cancer (BOX/Supplementary table S1 lists tens of E3s with documented cancer associations) and arises through genetic mutation, copy-number change, transcriptional dysregulation, and post-translational regulation.
- The same E3 can be oncogenic or tumour-suppressive depending on context. SPOP acts as a tumour suppressor in the nucleus but as an oncogene in the cytoplasm. NEDD4 suppresses tumours in normal cells but is oncogenic in RAS-mutant cells via PTEN degradation. CDH1 has dual-suppressor activity on BRAF, both via APC/C-driven proteolysis and via direct binding that prevents BRAF dimerization.
- Ubiquitin chain topology dictates outcome. K48 chains route substrates to the proteasome; K63 chains and monoubiquitylation regulate activity, localization or complex assembly without degradation (for example, SCF–SKP2-mediated K63-Ub of AKT activates AKT signalling; monoubiquitylation of KRAS at K147 or HRAS at K117 can enhance RAS signalling).
- Tumour-suppressor E3s whose loss drives cancer include FBXW7, BRCA1–BARD1, parkin/PARK2, and VHL. Oncogenic E3s whose hyperactivity drives cancer include MDM2, SCF–SKP2 in some contexts, and SPOP-cytoplasmic.
- BRCA1's E3 activity vs scaffolding role in tumour suppression is unresolved: a Brca1I26A mouse that retains BARD1 binding but loses ligase activity has reduced tumour development, while a Brca1C61G mouse with disrupted BARD1 binding phenocopies Brca1−/−. Both protein–protein scaffolding and ligase activity contribute.
- Therapeutic translation is already concrete: Nutlins/RG7112 disrupt MDM2–p53 and are in haematological-cancer trials; SCF–SKP2 inhibitors trigger p53-independent senescence in xenografts and reduce viability of p53-deficient cancers; PARP inhibitors exploit BRCA1/HR deficiency and USP11 levels predict resistance; PROTACs generalize the strategy by recruiting healthy E3s (for example, VHL, cereblon) to degrade undruggable oncoproteins.

## Limitations

- As a review, it provides synthesis rather than primary data; readers must follow citations for mechanistic detail and quantitative effect sizes.
- The "context-dependent" framing, while accurate, makes it hard to predict a priori whether a given E3 will be oncogenic or tumour-suppressive in a new cancer type, limiting prospective drug-target nomination from the framework alone.
- Many of the mouse-model conclusions rest on single-allele perturbations that may not capture compound mutations, lineage-specific cofactors, or microenvironmental effects.
- Therapeutic discussion is focused on E3–substrate disruption and PROTACs; deubiquitylating-enzyme (DUB) inhibition, ubiquitin-binding-domain inhibitors, and proteasome inhibition (beyond bortezomib mention) are treated more briefly.
- Cell-line and xenograft results dominate; clinical efficacy data for E3-targeted agents (other than proteasome inhibitors and emerging MDM2 antagonists) are limited.
- Coverage skews toward well-studied E3s (MDM2, BRCA1, FBXW7, VHL, parkin, SPOP); the long tail of less-characterized E3s with cancer association is mostly tabulated rather than mechanistically reviewed.

## Open questions

- Which contextual variables (lineage, signalling state, ubiquitin-chain topology preference, subcellular localization) most strongly predict whether an E3 acts as an oncogene or tumour suppressor in a given tumour, and can these be assayed prospectively?
- For BRCA1, what fraction of tumour-suppressor activity is contributed by ligase function versus scaffolding? Does this fraction differ across breast vs ovarian vs other lineages?
- Can selective inhibitors of cullin–RING E3s be developed that distinguish substrate-receptor modules (for example, FBXW7 vs SKP2 within SCF) without globally disrupting cullin function?
- How will PROTAC design choose among the dozens of available E3s as recruiters, and what determines which E3-target pairings yield productive ternary complexes?
- What is the role of non-degradative ubiquitylation (K63, K11, mono-ubiquitylation) in cancer, and is it druggable in ways that K48/proteasome biology is not?
- For E3s with substrate sets spanning both tumour suppressors and oncoproteins (for example, FBXW7 targeting MYC, cyclin E, mTOR), can substrate-selective inhibitors be engineered to dial in only the desired axis?
- How does the unfolded protein response and ER-stress arm of ubiquitylation feed back into tumour metabolism and therapy resistance?

## My take

This review's framing — that E3 cancer biology is fundamentally a substrate-context problem — is the right one and aligns with the current PROTAC-driven shift toward viewing E3s as a programmable resource rather than as drug targets per se. For ΩmegaWiki, the paper anchors the [[ubiquitin-ligase-e3]] concept and provides a mid-2010s consolidation of the E3-substrate axes (MDM2–p53, FBXW7–cyclin E, VHL–HIF, BRCA1–BARD1, SCF–SKP2–AKT/p27, NEDD4–PTEN, SPOP) that downstream papers — including drug-design papers targeting active PTM isoforms and structure-prediction work that enables E3–substrate modelling — depend on. The dual-role observations (NEDD4, SPOP, CDH1) are the most useful actionable point: they imply that any ubiquitin-targeted therapy must be evaluated in the genetic and signalling context of the tumour, not at the level of the E3 in isolation. The biggest extrapolation risk in the review is implicit: it treats each E3-substrate axis as separable, when in practice the cullin–RING substrate-receptor pool is in dynamic exchange and inhibiting one substrate receptor reroutes cullin activity to others. Future work — including modern proteomics and structural prediction for E3-substrate complexes — will be needed to test how cleanly the axes decouple.

## Definition

A magnetar giant flare (MGF) is a rare, very-high-luminosity (peak luminosity ~10^44–10^47 erg/s), short (~0.1 s main phase, ~hundreds of ms total) burst of hard X-rays and soft gamma-rays from a strongly magnetized, isolated neutron star (a magnetar, B ~ 10^15 G). The flare typically consists of a brief, intense initial peak followed by a longer pulsating tail modulated by the magnetar's spin period.

## Intuition

Magnetars store enormous magnetic free energy in their twisted, super-strong magnetospheres and crusts. When a crustquake or magnetospheric instability releases that energy on a sub-second timescale, the result is a fireball plus non-thermal emission carried by relativistic particles channeled along magnetic field lines — the giant flare. Because magnetars are nearby (galactic or local-group) and the energy is enormous, even individual events deliver enough photons to study fine timing and spectral structure.

## Formal notation

Energy budget: typical isotropic-equivalent energy release E ~ 10^44–10^47 erg over a main peak of duration delta_t ~ 0.1 s, plus a longer (~minutes) decaying tail modulated at the magnetar spin period (P ~ 0.3–12 s).

## Variants

- **Galactic giant flares** — three confirmed events from SGR 0526-66, SGR 1900+14, SGR 1806-20, observed in the late 20th and early 21st century.
- **Extragalactic giant flare candidates** — short, hard gamma-ray bursts whose properties (duration, spectrum, host galaxy) match a giant-flare interpretation rather than a compact-binary merger; GRB 200415A from the direction of NGC 253 is one such candidate.

## Comparison

Distinct from short gamma-ray bursts produced by compact binary mergers: giant flares lack the prompt-afterglow gravitational-wave signature, are typically shorter than a fraction of a second in main duration, and often have associated quasi-periodic oscillations.

## When to use

When discussing transient gamma-ray events whose host is a confirmed or suspected magnetar, or when modeling neutron-star magnetospheric energy release on sub-second timescales.

## Known limitations

- Sample size is tiny (a handful of confirmed events in five decades).
- Distinguishing extragalactic giant flares from short GRBs of compact-merger origin requires careful temporal-spectral analysis and is not always conclusive.

## Open problems

- What triggers the main burst — crustquake, magnetospheric instability, or both?
- What sets the population statistics of giant flares relative to ordinary magnetar bursts?
- How generic are kHz quasi-periodic oscillations in the main burst peak?

## Key papers

- [[very-high-frequency-oscillations-main-peak]] — first detection of significant kHz QPOs in the main peak of a magnetar giant flare (GRB 200415A toward NGC 253).

## My understanding

Magnetar giant flares sit at the intersection of strong-magnetic-field plasma physics and neutron-star interior physics: the timing structure carries information about magnetospheric dynamics (Alfvén waves, reconnection) while the spectral structure constrains photospheric and crustal conditions. The empirical bottleneck is instrumental — saturation in most detectors has historically prevented clean recording of the main burst phase, which is why ASIM-class instruments materially expand what is observable.
